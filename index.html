An Excerpt From Treasure Island

I remember him as if it were yesterday, as he came plodding to the inn door, his sea-chest following behind him in a hand-barrow—a tall, strong, heavy, nut-brown man, his tarry pigtail falling over the shoulder of his soiled blue coat, his hands ragged and scarred, with black, broken nails, and the sabre cut across one cheek, a dirty, livid white. I remember him looking round the cove and whistling to himself as he did so, and then breaking out in that old sea-song that he sang so often afterwards:

"Fifteen men on the dead man's chest—
Yo-ho-ho, and a bottle of rum!"

in the high, old tottering voice that seemed to have been tuned and broken at the capstan bars. Then he rapped on the door with a bit of stick like a handspike that he carried, and when my father appeared, called roughly for a glass of rum. This, when it was brought to him, he drank slowly, like a connoisseur, lingering on the taste and still looking about him at the cliffs and up at our signboard.

"This is a handy cove," says he at length; "and a pleasant sittyated grog-shop. Much company, mate?"

My father told him no, very little company, the more was the pity.

"Well, then," said he, "this is the berth for me. Here you, matey," he cried to the man who trundled his sea-chest, "bring up alongside. I'll stay here a bit."

And with that, he threw down three or four gold pieces upon the threshold. "You can tell me when I've worked through that," said he, looking as fierce as a commander.

Indeed, as he spoke, he looked around at us all, as if daring anyone to object. He was a man of few words when it came to pleasantries, but his presence was enough to command respect. His scarred face and weather-worn clothes told of battles fought on distant seas, and there was something in his eye that warned against crossing him.

My father hesitated but, seeing the gold, nodded in agreement. The strange seaman took his rum and sat by the fire, staring into the flames as if recalling some dark memory. I watched him carefully, fascinated yet wary. From that moment, the Admiral Benbow Inn was never quite the same, for with his arrival, a shadow had fallen over our quiet lives, and adventure, whether we sought it or not, had come knocking at our door.
